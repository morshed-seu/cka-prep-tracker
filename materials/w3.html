    Week 3 — Workloads, scheduling & app config

    This week is about what actually happens to your pods after you hit "create" — how they start up, how they get replaced during an update, how the scheduler picks a node for them, and how your app gets its configuration. It's the 15% "Workloads & Scheduling" domain on the exam blueprint, but it earns more than its weight: pod lifecycle and rollout mechanics are the vocabulary every week-6 troubleshooting task is written in. The pattern of the week: each kubectl abstraction (probe, rollout, taint, limit) is a thin veneer over one concrete mechanism underneath — a kubelet loop, two ReplicaSets, a scheduler filter, a cgroup file. Learn the mechanism and the veneer becomes obvious, instead of a wall of commands to memorize. Helm and Kustomize — the two most common tools for packaging and customizing Kubernetes YAML — close the week; both are directly examinable since the 2025 curriculum revision.

    Pod lifecycle internals

    3.1

    Phases vs container states vs conditions — three different status vocabularies

    Why it matters

    When a pod is misbehaving, Kubernetes actually tracks its health in three separate, overlapping fields — and mixing them up is the single most common way people confuse themselves while debugging. "Why is this pod not working?" is the exam's favorite question, and the answer is always written in .status — if you know which of the three vocabularies to read. People who conflate them chase ghosts: a pod can be phase Running while crash-looping, and CrashLoopBackOff isn't a phase at all — it doesn't even appear in the field you'd expect.

    The concept

    Pod status speaks three languages at once, at three levels — think of them as three different questions you could ask about the same pod, each with its own answer:

    VocabularyValuesWhere in status
    Phase — one word for the whole podPending, Running, Succeeded, Failed, Unknown.status.phase
    Container state — per containerWaiting (with a reason), Running (with startedAt), Terminated (with exitCode + reason).status.containerStatuses[].state
    Conditions — checklist of booleansPodScheduled, Initialized, ContainersReady, Ready.status.conditions[]

    The famous names you see in the kubectl get pods STATUS column — CrashLoopBackOff, ImagePullBackOff, ErrImagePull, ContainerCreating — feel like they should be one of the three vocabularies above, but they aren't officially any of them: they're Waiting reasons, a free-text explanation attached to a container's state, and kubectl just prints that reason in the STATUS column as a convenience because it's usually the most useful thing to show. Meanwhile the phase of a crash-looping pod stays Running the whole time — a pod counts as Running as long as at least one container is running or in the process of restarting, and a crash-looper is always doing one of those two things. The Ready condition is the one with real teeth: it's what a controller called the EndpointSlice controller reads to decide whether this pod should actually receive traffic sent to a Service (more on Services in week 4) — so a pod can look "up" in every casual sense and still be silently cut out of traffic because this one condition is False.

    Gotchas

    Unknown phase is a special case worth separating out immediately: it means the apiserver lost contact with the kubelet entirely — a node-level problem (the node might be down, or the network between it and the control plane broke), not anything to do with the pod's own containers. And a pod stuck Pending splits cleanly on the PodScheduled condition: False means the scheduler couldn't find it a node at all (read the condition's message for why); True but still Pending means it was placed on a node, and the kubelet there just hasn't gotten it running yet (image pull, volume mounting — read the pod's events to see which).

    3.2

    Init containers: serial, to completion, in order

    Why it matters

    Sometimes a container needs to wait for a dependency, or prep some files, before the real application starts — an init container is Kubernetes' built-in way to run that setup step first, guaranteed to finish before the app container ever starts. It's the standard "wait for X / prepare Y" tool, and a pod stuck in Init:0/2 is a classic troubleshooting prompt — you must know at a glance what that notation means and where to look next.

    The concept

    The kubelet (the node agent that actually starts and watches containers on each machine, introduced in week 1) runs initContainers strictly one at a time, in the order you list them, and each one must exit 0 — the standard Unix signal for "I succeeded" — before the next one starts; the app containers only start after the very last init container succeeds. If an init container fails, the kubelet re-runs that same init container according to the pod's restartPolicy (with Never, the whole pod gives up and goes Failed). The STATUS column narrates this progress step by step so you can watch it happen: Init:0/2 → Init:1/2 → PodInitializing → Running. One scheduling subtlety worth knowing: because init containers run before the app and never run alongside it, the pod's effective resource request (what the scheduler reserves room for, 3.16) is max(highest init request, sum of app requests) — so a fat init container can inflate what the scheduler thinks this pod costs, even though that container exits seconds after startup and never runs again.

    3.3

    Native sidecars: an init container with restartPolicy: Always

    Why it matters

    A sidecar is a helper container that rides alongside your main app in the same pod, doing some supporting job — shipping logs somewhere, proxying network traffic, that kind of thing. Sidecars are everywhere in real clusters, and the mechanism Kubernetes gives you to declare one properly is recent enough (GA, meaning officially stable and safe to depend on, in v1.33) to be attractive exam material. It comes down to one magic field with several behavioral consequences worth understanding rather than memorizing.

    The concept

    A native sidecar is simply an entry in initContainers that carries restartPolicy: Always — the only value that's legal to put there. That one field changes the contract in three ways: (1) at startup it keeps init containers' usual strict ordering, but it doesn't need to exit before the next container starts — it just needs to have started running (or pass a startup probe, 3.4, if it has one) before the pipeline moves on; (2) unlike a normal init container, it keeps running for the pod's entire life, restarting automatically if it ever dies, and it counts toward the pod's READY column just like a main container does; (3) at shutdown, sidecars are terminated after the main containers, and in reverse start order — so, for example, a log shipper is still alive to catch the app's very last lines before it, too, gets shut down. Before this feature existed, people ran sidecars as plain extra app containers, which caused two real problems: a proxy sidecar might race its own app at startup and not be ready in time, and in a Job (3.10), the sidecar running forever meant the Job never completed even after the real work finished. Both problems disappear with this one field — Jobs specifically treat native sidecars specially: the Job counts as complete once the main containers finish, regardless of whether the sidecar is still running.

    Lab
    1. A log tailer sidecar sharing an emptyDir with the app: 
      cat <<'EOF' | k apply -f -
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata: {name: sidecar-demo}
spec:
  volumes:
  - {name: logs, emptyDir: {}}
  initContainers:
  - name: tailer
    image: busybox
    restartPolicy: Always        # ← the whole feature
    command: [sh, -c, 'tail -F /logs/app.log']
    volumeMounts: [{name: logs, mountPath: /logs}]
  containers:
  - name: app
    image: busybox
    command: [sh, -c, 'while true; do date >> /logs/app.log; sleep 2; done']
    volumeMounts: [{name: logs, mountPath: /logs}]
EOF
sleep 10; k get pod sidecar-demo
      NAME           READY   STATUS    RESTARTS   AGE
sidecar-demo   2/2     Running   0          10s

      READY 2/2 — the "init" container is still running and counted. A normal init container would have shown 1/1.

    Gotchas

    Setting restartPolicy: OnFailure/Never on an init container is rejected at admission — meaning the apiserver refuses to create the object at all, before it ever reaches a node — because Always is the only legal value there, and only in initContainers (app containers, by contrast, can't have a per-container restartPolicy at all; that setting lives once, at the whole-pod level, 3.5). If a sidecar has a readiness probe, its readiness gates the pod's overall Ready condition too — a stuck sidecar can silently make the whole pod look not-ready.

    3.4

    The three probes and their three different consequences

    Why it matters

    A probe is just a periodic health check the kubelet runs against a container — "is this thing actually okay?" — and Kubernetes has three flavors that ask subtly different questions and react in completely different ways to a "no". "Add a probe to this deployment" is a straight exam task, and probe confusion is also the single most common self-inflicted production outage in real clusters. The three probes don't differ in how they check something — they differ in what happens on failure, and that's the part worth burning into memory.

    The concept

    All three probe types are executed by the kubelet on the pod's node, not by anything centralized: an httpGet probe makes an HTTP request that originates from the node's kubelet process itself, an exec probe runs a command inside the container via the CRI, and tcpSocket just tries to open a TCP connection and see if it succeeds. What differs between the three probe kinds — liveness, readiness, startup — is the consequence when the check fails:

    ProbeOn failureFailure symptom
    livenesskubelet kills the container; restartPolicy restarts itRESTARTS climbing, container state Terminated → Running cycles
    readinesspod's Ready condition → False; EndpointSlice controller removes its IP from ServicesREADY 0/1, no restarts, Service has fewer endpoints
    startupsuspends the other two until it first succeeds; if it never does within failureThreshold × periodSeconds, kill like livenessslow-starting app killed before it's up (when missing)

    All three probe kinds share the same tuning fields, just with different defaults tuned to their job: initialDelaySeconds (default 0 — wait this long after the container starts before the first check), periodSeconds (10 — how often to check), timeoutSeconds (1 — how long to wait for a response before counting it as a failure), failureThreshold (3 — how many failures in a row before acting), successThreshold (1). Do the arithmetic and a pattern falls out: an unconfigured probe declares failure only after roughly 30 seconds of consecutive bad checks (3 failures × a 10-second period) — not on the very first bad response, which is why a single slow request doesn't normally trigger anything.

    Gotchas

    Liveness probes on an overloaded app can create a death spiral, and it's worth walking through why: slow responses fail the liveness probe, the kubelet restarts the container thinking it's stuck, the restart briefly makes things even slower while it warms back up, and the cycle repeats — each restart making the underlying overload worse instead of better. Overload is fundamentally a readiness problem (shed the excess traffic elsewhere), never a liveness problem (restarting a healthy-but-busy process helps nothing). Also worth knowing: a pod with no readiness probe at all is considered Ready the instant it starts — an nginx that's up but serving nothing but 502 errors still counts as "ready" and still receives traffic, unless you've configured a probe that actually checks something meaningful about the app's health.

    3.5

    Graceful termination: SIGTERM, the grace period, and SIGKILL

    Why it matters

    Deleting a pod isn't instant, and understanding exactly what happens in between explains three everyday mysteries: why kubectl delete pod often takes about 30 seconds to return, why a killed container's exit code is so often 137, and why every serious production deployment has a small preStop sleep tucked into it. It also explains why $now (shorthand this course uses for --force --grace-period 0) exists as an escape hatch.

    The concept

    Deleting a pod is a negotiation, not an on/off kill switch. When you run kubectl delete pod, the apiserver doesn't actually remove the object right away — it just stamps a metadata.deletionTimestamp onto it, alongside a countdown called the grace period (terminationGracePeriodSeconds, 30 seconds by default). The kubelet notices that timestamp and, for each container in the pod: it first runs the container's preStop hook if one is defined (a command that gets to run before shutdown begins — and its time counts against the grace period, it doesn't get extra time on top), then it sends the container's process a SIGTERM — the standard Unix "please shut down" signal, aimed specifically at PID 1, the container's very first and main process. If the container is still alive once the whole grace period has elapsed, the kubelet gives up on asking nicely and sends SIGKILL, an unignorable "die immediately" signal, which shows up afterward as exit code 137 (that number isn't arbitrary — it's 128 plus signal number 9, the numeric ID of SIGKILL). Meanwhile, in parallel with all of this — not beforehand — a separate controller removes the pod from any Services routing to it. That parallelism creates a real race condition: a load balancer or proxy can still send a request to a pod that already received its SIGTERM and is in the middle of shutting down. The standard fix is a preStop hook that just does sleep 5: the pod keeps serving normally during that sleep while the "stop sending traffic here" news propagates around the cluster, and only after the sleep does the container actually get its SIGTERM. --force --grace-period 0 skips this whole negotiation: the API object is deleted immediately, without ever waiting for the kubelet to confirm the container actually stopped — fine for a throwaway lab pod you don't care about, genuinely dangerous for anything stateful, since two copies of the same process could briefly both believe they're the only one running.

    kubectl delete→deletionTimestamp set→preStop hook→SIGTERM → PID 1→grace period ticks (30s)→SIGKILL (exit 137)

    Each step only happens after the one before it finishes — this is a strict sequence, not several things racing in parallel.

    Lab
    1. A pod that handles SIGTERM exits fast: 
      k run polite --image=busybox -- sh -c 'trap "echo terminating; exit 0" TERM; while true; do sleep 1; done'
sleep 5; time k delete pod polite
      pod "polite" deleted

real    0m2.31s
    2. A pod that ignores SIGTERM burns the whole grace period: 
      k run rude --image=busybox -- sleep 3600
sleep 5; time k delete pod rude
      pod "rude" deleted

real    0m31.18s

      sleep as PID 1 has no TERM handler, and PID 1 ignores unhandled signals — so the kubelet waits 30s and SIGKILLs. This is why sh -c "…" wrapper entrypoints (PID 1 = sh, which doesn't forward signals) make apps take the full 30s to die.

    3. Tune the grace period and add a preStop hook: 
      cat <<'EOF' | k apply -f -
apiVersion: v1
kind: Pod
metadata: {name: hooked}
spec:
  terminationGracePeriodSeconds: 10
  containers:
  - name: c
    image: busybox
    command: [sleep, "3600"]
    lifecycle:
      preStop:
        exec: {command: [sh, -c, 'sleep 5']}
EOF
sleep 8; time k delete pod hooked
      pod "hooked" deleted

real    0m11.42s

      ~11s: 5s preStop + SIGTERM ignored + remaining grace → SIGKILL at the 10s mark.

    Verify

    The three timings ordered as predicted: ~2s (handler), ~31s (default grace), ~11s (10s grace incl. preStop).

    Gotchas

    terminationGracePeriodSeconds lives at pod spec level, once for the whole pod — not configurable per container. A pod stuck in Terminating forever usually means its node's kubelet is unreachable — the apiserver is waiting for a confirmation that will never come because nothing on that node can send it; that's the one case where reaching for --force is legitimate rather than a shortcut around a real problem. A quick exit-code decoder worth memorizing: 137 means SIGKILL (either an OOM kill, covered in 3.16, or the grace period simply ran out), while 143 means SIGTERM was received and handled cleanly (128 + 15, the signal number for SIGTERM).

    Deployments & rollouts

    3.6

    pod-template-hash: why every ReplicaSet name ends in gibberish

    Why it matters

    A Deployment is the object you normally use to run a set of replaceable pods and update them over time — but it never actually touches a pod directly, and once you see what it does instead, every rollout behavior stops feeling like magic. Surges, rollbacks, revision history — all of it becomes something you can inspect directly with k get rs rather than trust blindly.

    The concept

    A Deployment never manages pods directly. What it actually manages is ReplicaSets — a simpler controller whose only job is "keep exactly N copies of this exact pod template running" — one ReplicaSet per distinct pod template. The deployment controller computes a hash of the pod template (a short fingerprint derived from its contents) and stamps pod-template-hash=<hash> into three separate places at once: the ReplicaSet's name suffix, its label selector, and the labels on every pod it creates. Change anything in the template — even one field — and you get a different hash, which means the controller creates a brand new ReplicaSet for it and gradually shifts replicas from the old one to the new one (the mechanics of that shift are 3.7). The hash baked into the selector is what stops two ReplicaSets from ever fighting over the same pods — without it, two ReplicaSets both selecting on app=web would each think every matching pod belonged to them. Old ReplicaSets aren't deleted once they're replaced — they're just scaled down to 0 replicas and left sitting there: they are the revision history you see in kubectl rollout history, capped at a configurable number (revisionHistoryLimit, default 10, after which the oldest ones get pruned). A rollback, then, is not time travel in any special sense — it's simply the controller copying an old ReplicaSet's template back into the Deployment, which makes that old hash "current" again and scales that same old ReplicaSet back up while scaling the newer one down.

    Gotchas

    Scaling (k scale deployment web --replicas=5) does not create a new ReplicaSet or a new revision — the replica count field lives outside the pod template entirely, so changing it doesn't change the template's hash. And never edit or delete the auto-created ReplicaSets directly: the deployment controller is continuously reconciling toward what the Deployment says should exist, so it will simply undo whatever you changed and win the argument every time.

    3.7

    Watch a rolling update live: maxSurge and maxUnavailable in action

    Why it matters

    When a Deployment updates its pods, it doesn't kill all the old ones and start all the new ones at once — that would mean downtime. Instead it walks a careful path between two limits you control, and exam tasks describe that path in plain English: "update the image ensuring at most one pod is unavailable" is a maxUnavailable sentence in disguise. You need to be able to translate fluently between the English description and the YAML numbers, in both directions.

    The concept

    During a rolling update, the old ReplicaSet and the new one scale in opposite directions at the same time, kept in check by two constraints that both default to 25% of the desired replica count: maxSurge caps how many pods may exist above the desired count at any moment (that percentage rounds up), and maxUnavailable caps how many may be missing from the desired count (that percentage rounds down). Walk the arithmetic for 6 replicas at the defaults: surge = ceil(6 × 0.25) = ceil(1.5) = 2, so the total pod count never exceeds 8; unavailable = floor(1.5) = 1, so the ready count never drops below 5. Mechanically, the controller repeatedly scales the new ReplicaSet up to whatever the surge ceiling currently allows, waits for those new pods to report Ready, then scales the old ReplicaSet down to whatever the unavailable floor currently allows, and repeats this cycle until the new ReplicaSet is fully up and the old one is at zero. Readiness probes (3.4) are what makes any of this actually safe: a pod only counts as "available" for these calculations once it's Ready — so a genuinely broken new image, if it has no readiness probe telling Kubernetes otherwise, will sail through this whole process as if the rollout succeeded. Two corner cases worth knowing by name: maxUnavailable: 0 forces the rollout to be surge-only, which requires enough spare capacity in the cluster to hold the extra pods; and setting both to 0 is rejected outright, since that would make progress impossible.

    3.9

    Recreate strategy, and why StatefulSets & DaemonSets roll differently

    Why it matters

    Not every workload can safely run two versions of itself at once, and not every workload even has a notion of "surging" extra copies. Recognizing "this workload can't have two versions alive simultaneously" or "this workload is one-copy-per-node by design" tells you immediately which rollout rules — and which failure modes — actually apply to it.

    The concept

    Deployment, strategy Recreate: instead of the gradual surge/unavailable dance from 3.7, this kills every old pod first, waits for them all to be gone, and only then creates all the new ones — a real downtime window, but the guarantee in exchange is that two versions are never running at the same time. You reach for this specifically when versions genuinely can't coexist: think a volume that only one pod can mount at a time (a ReadWriteOnce volume, covered in week 5, both versions would need), a database schema migration where old and new code disagree about the schema, or any singleton process holding an exclusive lock. StatefulSet (with updateStrategy: RollingUpdate) updates pods one at a time, and specifically in reverse order — highest numbered pod first (web-2, then web-1, then web-0) — and each one must be fully Ready before the next is even touched. This preserves each pod's stable identity throughout and never creates a surge pod — there's no such thing as a temporary "web-1a" sitting alongside "web-1". Its partition field doubles as a built-in canary mechanism: set it to some number N, and only pods with an ordinal ≥ N get updated, letting you test the new version on just the highest-numbered replicas first. DaemonSet runs exactly one pod per matching node, so the whole concept of "surging" extra copies doesn't apply at all — instead, the controller deletes the pod on a given node and recreates the new version on that same node, working through maxUnavailable (default 1) nodes at a time. Both StatefulSet and DaemonSet also support a third option, updateStrategy: OnDelete: with this set, the controller updates nothing on its own at all — it waits for you to manually delete each old pod, entirely by hand, before replacing it.

    3.11

    Taints & tolerations: the three effects, and the one that evicts

    Why it matters

    A taint is a marker you put on a node that says "repel pods by default, unless a pod specifically says it's okay with this" — the opposite of an attractive label, and it's the mechanism behind both "why won't anything schedule on this node?" and "why did my pods suddenly disappear from node X?". Taints aren't just a user-facing feature either — Kubernetes uses this exact same mechanism internally: control-plane isolation (2.4) and automatic dead-node eviction are both just taints under the hood, applied by the system instead of by you.

    The concept

    First, the map this whole group hangs on. Week 1 gave you the scheduler framework in broad strokes; here's exactly where each control you're about to learn in this group plugs into that framework — the diagram below walks the scheduler's decision process left to right, from a queue of unplaced pods through hard yes/no filtering, to soft scoring among the survivors, to finally binding a pod to a node:

    Now taints themselves, in detail. A taint lives entirely on the node side and has the shape key=value:effect — it's the node saying "here's a condition, and here's what I do to pods that don't tolerate it." A toleration, on the pod side, only neutralizes a matching taint — it never actively pulls a pod toward that node (pulling a pod somewhere is a completely different mechanism, node affinity, covered next in 3.12). The three possible effects escalate in severity: PreferNoSchedule is the mildest — just a soft nudge to avoid the node if a better option exists (this happens at Score time, not Filter time); NoSchedule is a hard Filter that blocks new pods from landing there, but pods already running on the node when the taint appears are left completely alone; NoExecute is the most severe — a hard Filter for new pods, plus active eviction of any already-running pods that don't tolerate it, enforced by a background component called the taint manager. A toleration for a NoExecute taint can also carry tolerationSeconds, effectively saying "I'll tolerate this for N seconds, then leave" rather than tolerating it forever. Kubernetes' own internals run on exactly this mechanism: the control-plane node carries node-role.kubernetes.io/control-plane:NoSchedule (2.4), and the way a dead node gets handled is the node lifecycle controller adding node.kubernetes.io/unreachable:NoExecute to it — and every pod in the cluster silently carries a built-in 300-second toleration for that specific taint, which is exactly why pods stranded on a node that's gone unreachable take a full 5 minutes to get rescheduled elsewhere, not zero.

    3.12

    nodeSelector vs node affinity: hard filter vs weighted score

    Why it matters

    "Schedule this pod on a node with label X" is one of the most literal, recurring exam tasks — you attach labels to nodes (say, disk=ssd) and then tell the scheduler which nodes a pod is allowed to land on based on those labels. The skill actually being tested is writing the affinity YAML shape from memory, since it's one of the most deeply nested specs you'll meet on the exam.

    The concept

    Three tools exist for the same underlying decision — "which nodes are acceptable for this pod?" (see the scheduler diagram in 3.11 for where each one plugs in): nodeSelector is the simplest — a flat key:value match, a pure hard Filter, just three lines of YAML; reach for it whenever the task doesn't say anything about "preferring" one node over another, since a plain requirement is all it can express. requiredDuringSchedulingIgnoredDuringExecution node affinity is also a hard Filter, functionally the same "yes or no" as nodeSelector, but written with richer expressions (In, NotIn, Exists, Gt, and more) instead of a flat equality check; multiple matchExpressions inside one term are ANDed together (all must match), while multiple nodeSelectorTerms are ORed together (any one term matching is enough). preferredDuringSchedulingIgnoredDuringExecution is different in kind, not just in syntax — it's a Score-time nudge carrying a weight from 1–100, so the pod still schedules somewhere even if no node satisfies the preference at all; it just influences which node wins among the candidates that already passed the hard filters. The long suffix "IgnoredDuringExecution" on both affinity forms is a promise worth reading completely literally: the rule is checked only at the moment of scheduling — if a node's labels change afterward, any pod already running there stays put, unaffected.

    3.13

    Pod anti-affinity: spreading replicas, and what topologyKey really means

    Why it matters

    "Ensure no two replicas run on the same node" — for high availability, so one node failure can't take out every copy of your app at once — is a classic task, and topologyKey is the field people tend to wave their hands about without really understanding. It actually has a precise meaning that's easy to hold onto once you see it.

    The concept

    Pod (anti-)affinity differs from node affinity (3.12) in a fundamental way: instead of matching against a node's own labels, its labelSelector matches other pods — "keep me away from (or near) pods that look like this." topologyKey then names a node label that defines what "together" even means for that comparison. The way to picture it: take every node in the cluster and sort them into buckets, one bucket per distinct value of whatever label topologyKey names; anti-affinity then means "don't place me in the same bucket as a matching pod." Set topologyKey: kubernetes.io/hostname and every node becomes its own bucket (since every node has a unique hostname), so the rule reads as "not on the same node." Set it to topology.kubernetes.io/zone instead, and the rule becomes "not in the same availability zone," since now all the nodes sharing a zone label count as one bucket. Like node affinity, this comes in the same two flavors: required (a hard Filter — the pod simply won't schedule if it can't be satisfied) and preferred (a soft Score-time nudge).     3.14

    Priority & preemption: how the scheduler chooses victims

    Why it matters

    Not every pod deserves equal treatment when the cluster runs short on room — priority is how you tell the scheduler that some pods matter more than others, up to and including evicting a less-important pod to make space. It explains two things you'll meet in troubleshooting: why your pod was suddenly evicted even though the node wasn't under memory or disk pressure at all, and how critical system components always seem to find room no matter how full a cluster gets. It's also a tidy, self-contained "create a PriorityClass and use it" exam task.

    The concept

    A PriorityClass is a small cluster-scoped object — nothing more than a name bound to an integer value, plus a couple of optional fields (globalDefault, preemptionPolicy). Pods reference a PriorityClass by name in their spec, and an admission controller (a piece of apiserver logic that inspects and can modify objects as they're created, covered more in week 7) copies that integer into the pod's own pod.spec.priority field. That number acts in two separate places in the scheduling flow (see 3.11's diagram): first in the queue, where higher-priority pods simply get considered for scheduling before lower-priority ones; and second in a step called PostFilter, which only runs when a high-priority pod fails every node's filters — the scheduler responds by simulating the removal of lower-priority pods, node by node, until it finds one where removing some subset would let the high-priority pod fit. It then actually evicts that minimal set of victims (still respecting each victim's normal graceful termination from 3.5, not killing them instantly) and writes the winning node's name into the waiting pod's status.nominatedNodeName field. That nomination is a hint, not a guarantee: the pod goes back into the scheduling queue and could still end up landing somewhere else entirely if conditions change before it's actually placed. Two built-in PriorityClasses sit far above anything you'd normally create: system-cluster-critical (value 2000000000) and system-node-critical (2000001000) — this is the exact mechanism by which components like kube-proxy and CoreDNS essentially never lose a scheduling fight against ordinary application pods.

    3.15

    Cordon, drain, uncordon — fluently, and knowing what each really does

    Why it matters

    When a node needs maintenance — a kernel update, a hardware check, anything that means it should stop taking work temporarily — you need a way to first stop new pods from landing there and then safely move the ones already there off. You already ran drains inside week 2's upgrade drill without dwelling on the mechanism; this lesson makes that mechanism explicit and the commands a reflex, because node-maintenance tasks are near-guaranteed on the exam and worth easy points once they're automatic.

    The concept

    Cordon is really just one boolean flag. Running k cordon node02 sets spec.unschedulable: true on the Node object, and a controller mirrors that setting as a node.kubernetes.io/unschedulable:NoSchedule taint (the same taint mechanism from 3.11) behind the scenes. It's purely a hard Filter (see the diagram in 3.11): brand-new pods avoid the node from that point on, but pods already running there are left completely untouched — cordon on its own moves nothing. Drain is cordon plus active evictions. The kubectl drain command first cordons the node exactly as above (and this cordoning step happens client-side, in kubectl itself, not as some special server feature), then creates an Eviction request for every pod still running there — the same polite, graceful-termination delete that respects PodDisruptionBudgets, rather than a blunt force-delete. Three categories of pods make drain refuse to proceed by default, each with its own override flag, and each refusal exists for a good reason: DaemonSet pods (--ignore-daemonsets — pointless to evict, since they'd be recreated on that same node instantly, and they already tolerate the cordon taint anyway), pods using an emptyDir volume (--delete-emptydir-data — because that data is genuinely destroyed the moment the pod is evicted, and drain wants you to explicitly acknowledge that), and bare pods with no controller managing them (--force — because nothing will ever recreate them once gone, unlike a Deployment's pods). Uncordon simply clears the boolean back to schedulable — and importantly, nothing moves back on its own: pods that were evicted during the drain got rescheduled onto other nodes at the time, and they stay wherever they landed even after the original node comes back.

    3.16

    Requests vs limits: scheduler bookkeeping vs cgroup enforcement

    Why it matters

    Requests and limits look like a matched pair in the YAML, but they do genuinely different jobs, and the asymmetry between them explains half of all container mysteries: go over your CPU limit and you just get slow; go over your memory limit and your container gets killed outright. That asymmetry is the root cause behind the exam's beloved exit code 137.

    The concept

    The two fields live in completely different worlds. Requests exist only in the scheduler's ledger — a bookkeeping exercise, not a runtime mechanism at all: "does this pod's requests fit in the node's remaining free allocatable capacity" is a hard Filter (see the diagram in 3.11), computed purely by summing up other pods' declared requests on that node, never from what those pods are actually measured using in practice. That means a node crammed with idle pods that each declared fat requests reads as completely "full" to the scheduler, even if every one of those pods is doing nothing. Limits, by contrast, are real runtime enforcement — the kubelet translates them into actual files inside a Linux kernel mechanism called cgroups (control groups — the kernel feature that lets you cap how much CPU or memory a group of processes can use) on the node:

    Spec fieldcgroup fileEnforcement
    requests.cpu: 500mcpu.weightproportional share under contention only — no cap
    limits.cpu: 500mcpu.max = 50000 100000CFS quota: 50ms runtime per 100ms period → throttling, never killing
    limits.memory: 64Mimemory.maxexceed → kernel OOM killer → exit code 137, reason OOMKilled
    requests.memory(bookkeeping + eviction ranking, 3.18)no runtime enforcement

    3.17

    QoS classes: derived, not declared — and what they buy you at OOM time

    Why it matters

    When a node genuinely runs out of memory, something has to die first, and QoS class (Quality of Service — a priority tier Kubernetes assigns every pod) decides the sacrifice order. Exam questions probe specifically whether you know these classes are computed from your requests and limits — there's no qos: field anywhere to set directly; you get whichever class your resource spec happens to imply.

    The concept

    The apiserver derives one of three classes from the pod's resource spec at creation time and records the result in status.qosClass — again, purely computed, never something you write yourself:

    ClassRuleoom_score_adj (kernel OOM priority)
    Guaranteedevery container: requests == limits for both cpu and memory-997 — nearly untouchable
    Burstableat least one request or limit set, but not Guaranteed2–999, scaled down as memory request grows (≈ 1000 − 1000×memRequest/nodeMem)
    BestEffortno requests, no limits anywhere1000 — first against the wall

    The kubelet plants a number called oom_score_adj onto every container process specifically so that if the kernel itself ends up OOM-killing something — meaning the whole node runs out of memory faster than the kubelet's own eviction logic can react — there's already a clear pecking order in place: BestEffort pods die first, then Burstable pods (with the ones that requested the least memory going first among those), and Guaranteed pods die only as an absolute last resort. The kubelet's own eviction ranking, covered next in 3.18, is a related but genuinely distinct mechanism — it ranks candidates by whether their actual usage exceeds their requests, then by priority (3.14), then by how far over their requests they are — and QoS only influences that ranking indirectly, since a BestEffort pod (having requested essentially nothing) is by definition always "over" its own requests.

    3.18

    Two evictions that share a name: node-pressure vs API-initiated

    Why it matters

    The phrase "pod was evicted" actually points to two completely different mechanisms, with two completely different diagnoses, depending on which machinery did the evicting. Being able to tell them apart at a glance — from just a couple of clues — is a genuine week-6 troubleshooting superpower.

    The concept

    Node-pressure eviction is the kubelet defending its own node from running out of a critical resource, acting entirely on its own initiative. It continuously watches a handful of signals against configured thresholds — the defaults are memory.available < 100Mi, nodefs.available < 10%, imagefs.available < 15%, and nodefs.inodesFree < 5% — and once one is breached, it goes through a fixed sequence: first it sets a node condition (MemoryPressure or DiskPressure, which also gets mirrored as a NoSchedule taint, 3.11, so new pods stop landing there too), then it tries to reclaim space on its own (running image garbage collection first, for disk pressure specifically), and only then does it start killing pods — ranked first by whether their actual usage exceeds their declared requests, then by pod priority (3.14), then by how far over their requests they are. Crucially, this whole process is a purely local kubelet decision: there's no Eviction API call involved at all, PodDisruptionBudgets are completely ignored, and pods killed once a hard threshold is breached get no grace period whatsoever. The evidence is left behind for you to find: the pod object itself survives as Failed, with reason Evicted. API-initiated eviction is the other mechanism — it's what drain (3.15) actually does under the hood: it creates a proper Eviction API object per pod, which is a polite delete that does respect PodDisruptionBudgets and goes through full graceful termination (3.5), and which the apiserver will refuse with an HTTP 429 status if the relevant budget has no room left to give.

    3.19

    Install metrics-server and understand the aggregated API it rides on

    Why it matters

    kubectl top is your bread-and-butter resource-troubleshooting instrument — it shows live CPU and memory usage for nodes and pods — and it's exam-relevant on its own. But installing the component behind it, metrics-server, is also the one place in this course where you'll actually touch the API aggregation layer and the front-proxy certificates kubeadm generated back in week 2, rather than just reading about them.

    The concept

    kubectl top queries an API group called metrics.k8s.io — and the apiserver itself has no built-in implementation of that group at all. Instead, an APIService object tells the apiserver "whenever a request comes in for this group, delegate it to a Service running somewhere in the cluster": concretely, the apiserver proxies the request over to the metrics-server pod, authenticating itself to that pod using the front-proxy-client certificate — this is precisely what the third kubeadm CA from week 2 exists for, and metrics-server checks that certificate against what's called the requestheader CA to confirm the request really did come from a trusted apiserver. Metrics-server, in turn, does its own scraping: it hits every kubelet's /metrics/resource endpoint, on port 10250, over TLS. And this is exactly where lab friction shows up: kubeadm-built kubelets serve self-signed TLS certificates by default, which metrics-server has no way to verify against any CA it trusts — hence the well-known --kubelet-insecure-tls flag used to work around it in labs (a production cluster fixes this properly instead, by rotating kubelets onto serving certificates actually signed by the cluster's CA).

    kubectl top→apiserver→APIService metrics.k8s.io → proxy w/ front-proxy cert→metrics-server pod→every kubelet :10250/metrics/resource

    The request hops from your terminal, through the apiserver's proxy, to metrics-server, which itself hops out to every node's kubelet to actually collect the numbers.

    3.20

    HPA: the control loop, the formula, and a real load test

    Why it matters

    The HorizontalPodAutoscaler (HPA) automatically changes a Deployment's replica count for you, based on measured load, instead of you having to watch a dashboard and scale it by hand. "Create an HPA that scales deployment X between 1 and 10 replicas at 50% CPU" is a one-liner worth full exam points — if you also understand why it scales up fast, scales down slow, and never quite fires exactly when you'd naively expect.

    The concept

    The HPA controller — a loop running inside kube-controller-manager, the same component family from week 1 — wakes up every 15 seconds, reads current pod metrics from metrics.k8s.io (the API group metrics-server serves, 3.19), and computes a target replica count with one formula: desired = ceil(current × actualUtilization / targetUtilization). That "utilization" is always measured as a percentage of requests, not of the node's raw capacity — so an HPA pointed at a deployment whose containers never declared a cpu request fails immediately with "missing request for cpu", because there's nothing to compute a percentage against. A ±10% tolerance band around the target suppresses small jitter so the controller doesn't twitch the replica count back and forth over noise (and since v1.33 that tolerance is configurable per-HPA via spec.behavior). The asymmetry between scaling up and down is entirely deliberate, not an accident of the algorithm: scaling up acts immediately, with effectively no waiting period (a stabilization window of 0 seconds), because a real load spike needs a fast response — but scaling down looks at the highest recommendation seen over a full 300-second stabilization window before acting, so a brief lull doesn't trigger a premature scale-down that then has to reverse itself moments later. The practical upshot: load spikes get answered within seconds, but the cluster insists on five minutes of sustained calm before it lets replicas drop. Use the autoscaling/v2 API version for any hand-written HPA YAML — the older v1 can only scale on CPU and lacks the richer behavior configuration entirely.

    3.21

    Consume a ConfigMap (and a Secret) all four ways

    Why it matters

    A ConfigMap holds plain configuration data (like a database hostname) separately from your application's image, so the same image can run with different settings in different environments — and a Secret is the same idea for sensitive values. Config plumbing like this appears in roughly half the exam's workload tasks as a sub-step ("the app reads DB_HOST from the ConfigMap…"), so being fluent in all four ways a pod can consume one means this is never the part where you slow down.

    The concept

    A ConfigMap is, underneath everything, just a map of string keys to string values sitting in etcd (the cluster's data store, week 1) — nothing fancier. The four ways a pod can consume one differ only in who injects the data into the running container, and when that happens: (1) env.valueFrom.configMapKeyRef pulls one specific key into one specific environment variable; (2) envFrom.configMapRef dumps every key in the ConfigMap into its own environment variable at once (optionally with a shared prefix added to each name); (3) mounting the ConfigMap as a configMap volume turns every key into its own file inside the container, written by the kubelet at the moment the volume is mounted; (4) command/args interpolation lets you write $(VAR) directly inside a command: or args: list, and Kubernetes expands it using whatever environment variables paths (1) or (2) already defined, at container start. Secrets mirror all four of these exact shapes, just swapping in secretKeyRef, secretRef, and secret: volumes respectively — the only real differences are that Secret data is stored base64-encoded rather than as plain text (3.23 covers exactly what protection that does and doesn't provide), and that Secret volume mounts are backed by tmpfs (an in-memory filesystem, so the decoded values never touch the node's disk).

    3.22

    Update propagation: volumes update, env never, subPath never

    Why it matters

    "I changed the ConfigMap, why does my app still use the old config?" is both a real-life classic frustration and prime exam-trap material. The answer isn't mysterious at all once you know the mechanism — it's entirely mechanical, and it follows directly from the symlink you saw in 3.21's lab.

    The concept

    Three rules, all of them direct consequences of that ..data symlink discovery from 3.21. Mounted ConfigMaps and Secrets update live, without restarting anything: the kubelet periodically re-syncs in the background, writes the new contents into a brand-new timestamped directory alongside the old one, and then flips the ..data symlink to point at the new directory atomically — meaning consumers reading through that symlink never catch it in a half-updated, inconsistent state, and the whole change typically lands within about a minute (limited by how often the kubelet re-syncs and its internal cache). Environment variables never update, ever: their values were copied into the container's process environment once, at the moment the container was created, and nothing about how a running process's environment works allows that to change later — only creating a new container (via a restart) re-reads the current values. subPath mounts never update either, but for a different, more surprising reason: setting subPath: COLOR bind-mounts that one specific file directly into the container, which completely bypasses the ..data symlink indirection — so that bind mount just keeps pointing at whatever the old timestamped directory contained, forever, even though the plain (non-subPath) mount right next to it updates fine. The two standard escape hatches when you actually need a config change to take effect everywhere: run kubectl rollout restart right after changing config (forces new containers, which pick up fresh env vars and fresh subPath contents), or use Kustomize's configMapGenerator (3.26), which renames the ConfigMap itself whenever its content changes, so a rollout happens automatically as a side effect of that rename.

    Gotchas

And remember which direction the trap usually runs: it's essentially never "the volume mount went stale" — it's almost always the env var or the subPath mount that's frozen while everyone assumes the whole pod should have picked up the change.

    3.23

    Secrets are base64, not encryption — and what encryption at rest actually is

    Why it matters

    The word "Secret" is genuinely misleading if you take it at face value, and the exam expects sober, precise knowledge here: create and consume Secrets fast, know exactly what security they do not provide by default, and recognize the shape of an EncryptionConfiguration file when a task or a troubleshooting scenario shows you one.

    The concept

    Base64 is an encoding, not encryption at all — it's just a reversible way to represent arbitrary bytes as printable text, with zero secrecy provided; anyone can decode it in one command, as the lab below demonstrates. By default, a Secret sits in etcd as a plain, unencrypted record containing those bytes — which means anyone with direct access to etcd (or a copy of the etcd snapshot you took back in week 2!) can read every single secret in the entire cluster, no Kubernetes credentials required at all. The real protections available are: RBAC (Role-Based Access Control, week 7's keycard-style permissions system) restricting who's allowed to call the Secrets API in the first place, ordinary filesystem protection of the etcd data directory itself, and a feature called encryption at rest — an apiserver-side layer configured by a file called EncryptionConfiguration, passed to the apiserver via the --encryption-provider-config flag. That file lists which resources to encrypt and an ordered list of providers to use (aescbc, secretbox, kms, or identity, which just means "store as plaintext"): the first provider in that list is used to encrypt every new write; every provider after it can still decrypt data that was already encrypted with it, but is never used for new writes. Objects that already existed in etcd before encryption was turned on stay in their old, unencrypted form until something actually rewrites them — which is exactly why turning on encryption for the first time always ends with a deliberate mass-rewrite command to bring existing data into the new encrypted form.

    Secrets have two ways to set values that are easy to confuse: stringData: lets you write plain, unencoded text and the apiserver base64-encodes it for you automatically, while data: expects you to have already base64-encoded the value yourself — mixing the two up (or double-applying the encoding) produces garbage that decodes into more garbage. When you're manually encoding something, echo -n matters more than it looks: plain echo secret | base64 silently includes a trailing newline character in what gets encoded, so the decoded password ends up with an invisible newline stuck on the end that breaks logins in confusing ways. And it's worth being precise about what encryption at rest actually protects: it protects etcd's data on disk, not the Kubernetes API itself — anyone who's RBAC-allowed to run kubectl get secrets still sees the plaintext value through the API exactly as before, and that's the intended, correct behavior, not a bug or a gap in the feature.

    Helm & Kustomize

    3.24

    The Helm command set, drilled on a real chart

    Why it matters

    Helm is the most widely used package manager for Kubernetes — a way to install a whole bundle of related YAML (a database, its config, its Service, all of it) as one named unit, with knobs you can tune without editing the YAML directly. It's explicitly named in the post-2025 curriculum, and helm.sh/docs is one of your three exam-allowed documentation sites. The exam expects operational fluency here — install, override, upgrade, roll back — not chart authoring; you're not expected to write Helm templates yourself.

    The concept

    Helm 3 has no cluster-side component at all (an earlier server-side piece called Tiller existed in Helm 2 and was removed): the Helm client does all the work locally, rendering a chart's Go templates (a templating syntax that fills in placeholders with your values) against whatever values you supply, and then simply applies the resulting plain YAML to the cluster, the same as kubectl apply would. A chart is the packaged bundle of templates, a release is one specific, named installation of that chart into a cluster, and all the state describing a release's history lives inside the cluster itself, stored as ordinary Kubernetes Secrets named sh.helm.release.v1.<release>.v<N> in whatever namespace the release lives in — one such Secret per revision. That's the entire mechanism behind both helm history (it's just listing those Secrets) and helm rollback (it re-applies the contents of an older one). Values that customize a chart flow through three layers, lowest to highest precedence: the chart's own built-in defaults, then anything in a -f values.yaml file you supply, then any --set key=value flags on the command line, which win over everything else. helm template renders the final YAML locally without ever touching the cluster at all — it's your go-to "what would this actually create?" debugging tool before you commit to an install or upgrade.

    3.26

    Kustomize: a base, an overlay, and kubectl apply -k

    Why it matters

    Kustomize is the curriculum's other main configuration-customization tool, alongside Helm — and unlike Helm, it's built directly into kubectl, so there's nothing separate to install, just the -k flag. The exam shape here is typically: "apply this kustomization" or "make overlay X change the image, replica count, or namespace" of some base set of manifests.

    The concept

    Where Helm works by templating text (filling placeholders into a template before it's even valid YAML), Kustomize takes a fundamentally different approach: it transforms already-valid Kubernetes objects. A kustomization.yaml file names a set of input resources (plain YAML manifests) and a pipeline of declarative transformations to apply to them — there are no variables and no template syntax anywhere; it's plain YAML going in, and plain YAML coming out the other side. A base is a self-contained kustomization that stands on its own; an overlay is a separate kustomization that imports that base (resources: [../../base]) and layers additional changes on top of it without ever editing the base's own files: cross-cutting fields like namespace, namePrefix, and commonLabels that apply broadly, targeted patches for specific field-level edits (either strategic-merge style or JSON6902 style, and Kustomize auto-detects which one you wrote), and dedicated transformers for common jobs like images (retag a container image without touching the manifest text itself) and replicas. One note if you're studying from older material: the field used to be called patchesStrategicMerge:, but that's been deprecated since kustomize v5 — the current field is just patches:. The one generator genuinely worth knowing well: configMapGenerator creates ConfigMaps whose names carry a content-hash suffix computed from their data, and it automatically rewrites every reference to that ConfigMap elsewhere in the manifests to match — so any time the content changes, the name changes too, and every pod referencing it gets a new name to react to, which forces a rollout automatically. That's the designed, structural fix for the exact frozen-env-var problem you saw in 3.22.

    Lab
    1. Build the base: 
      mkdir -p ~/kapp/base ~/kapp/overlays/prod
cat <<'EOF' > ~/kapp/base/deployment.yaml
apiVersion: apps/v1

metadata: {name: web}
spec:
  replicas: 2
  selector: {matchLabels: {app: web}}
  template:
    metadata: {labels: {app: web}}
    spec:
      containers: [{name: nginx, image: nginx:1.27}]
EOF
cat <<'EOF' > ~/kapp/base/kustomization.yaml

kind: Kustomization
resources:
- deployment.yaml
EOF
    2. Build the prod overlay — namespace, name prefix, a strategic-merge patch, an image retag: 
      cat <<'EOF' > ~/kapp/overlays/prod/replicas.yaml

kind: Deployment
metadata: {name: web}
spec:
  replicas: 4
EOF
cat <<'EOF' > ~/kapp/overlays/prod/kustomization.yaml

  newTag: "1.29"

      k kustomize ~/kapp/overlays/prod
      apiVersion: apps/v1

  name: prod-web

  replicas: 4
  ...
      containers:

    4. See configMapGenerator close the 3.22 loop: 
      cd ~/kapp/overlays/prod

    k kustomize output matched all four expectations before you applied, and k get deploy -n prod confirmed after.

    Gotchas

    kubectl apply -k dir/ takes the path to the directory containing kustomization.yaml, not the file itself — using -f pointed at a kustomization.yaml directly is a completely different (and wrong) thing to do. Setting namespace: inside a kustomization doesn't create that namespace for you — it just stamps every resource with it, and the namespace itself still needs to exist beforehand, or the apply will fail. And Kustomize's automatic name-reference rewriting only works for references it actually understands structurally (like a Deployment's own selector, or a volume mount referencing a ConfigMap by name) — a resource name mentioned as plain text inside a container's args string, for instance, won't get updated when namePrefix or a generator's content-hash renames the object it's supposed to be pointing at.

    3.27

    CRDs & operators: install one, inspect it, recognize the pattern

    Why it matters

    Kubernetes lets you teach its API entirely new object types that behave just like the built-in ones — kubectl get, RBAC, everything — without ever touching the apiserver's own source code. "Custom Resource Definitions and operators" is a named 2025-revision curriculum bullet, tested at the understand and use level: install an operator, find the CRDs it added, create a custom resource with one — not write a controller yourself.

    The concept

    A CRD (CustomResourceDefinition) teaches the apiserver a new resource type: post a single CustomResourceDefinition object, and the API instantly grows a full set of real endpoints for that new type — storage in etcd, RBAC enforcement, kubectl get support, even kubectl explain field documentation (a CRD carries a schema describing its own fields, the same way built-in types do) — but with zero actual behavior attached to it yet. All of that machinery just gives you a place to store and read structured data; nothing reacts to it on its own. Behavior comes from a separate piece: a controller, a program watching those custom objects and continuously reconciling the real world toward whatever they describe — the exact same watch/reconcile pattern from week 1's deployment controller, just applied to a type nobody but you and this controller know about. An operator is simply the bundle of both pieces together: one or more CRDs plus the controller Deployment that gives them meaning, usually installed from one manifest URL or one Helm chart. The punchline worth sitting with: this is exactly the architecture you already learned in week 1, reused — a built-in Deployment plus the deployment-controller, and a custom Certificate plus cert-manager (the operator you'll install below), are the same watch/reconcile loop, running the same way, one built directly into Kubernetes and one you installed yourself. The API machinery treats both identically, which is exactly why kubectl works flawlessly on resource types that didn't even exist in the cluster yesterday.

    Week 3 recall quiz

    Answer out loud before revealing. If any answer surprises you, redo that lesson's lab.

    1. A pod shows CrashLoopBackOff. What is its phase, and where exactly does that string live in the status YAML?
      Answer

      Phase is Running (restarting counts). The string is a container Waiting reason: .status.containerStatuses[].state.waiting.reason. Phases, container states and conditions are three separate vocabularies (3.1).

    2. What single field turns an init container into a sidecar, and in what order do sidecars stop at pod shutdown?
      Answer

      restartPolicy: Always on an initContainers entry (the only value allowed there; GA in v1.33). At shutdown they stop after the main containers, in reverse start order — so a log shipper sees the app's final output (3.3).

    3. Failing readiness probe vs failing liveness probe — give the observable signature of each.
      Answer

      Readiness: READY 0/1, zero restarts, pod IP removed from Service EndpointSlices. Liveness: RESTARTS climbing (kubelet kills, restartPolicy restarts). Overloaded apps need readiness (shed traffic), never liveness (restart storm) (3.4).

    4. Why does a container whose entrypoint is sh -c "myapp" typically take exactly 30 seconds to terminate, and with what exit code?
      Answer

      PID 1 is sh, which doesn't forward SIGTERM and, as PID 1, ignores unhandled signals — so nothing reacts until the grace period (default 30s) expires and the kubelet sends SIGKILL: exit code 137 (128+9) (3.5).

    5. What three places carry the pod-template-hash, and what does a rollback actually do?
      Answer

      The ReplicaSet's name suffix, its selector, and the pods' labels — preventing RSes from adopting each other's pods. Rollback copies an old (retained, scaled-to-0) RS's template back into the Deployment, scaling that RS up again under a new revision number (3.6, 3.8).

    6. A 6-replica deployment rolls with default strategy. Maximum total pods, minimum ready pods, and the rounding rule?
      Answer

      maxSurge 25% of 6 = 1.5, rounded up → 2 → max 8 total. maxUnavailable 25% = 1.5, rounded down → 1 → min 5 ready (3.7).

    7. NoSchedule vs NoExecute — and why do pods on an unreachable node move only after ~5 minutes?
      Answer

      NoSchedule filters new placements only; NoExecute also evicts running pods that don't tolerate it. Dead nodes get node.kubernetes.io/unreachable:NoExecute, and every pod carries an automatic toleration for it with tolerationSeconds: 300 — hence the 5-minute delay (3.11).

    8. Define topologyKey in one sentence, and say what topologyKey: kubernetes.io/hostname means for anti-affinity.
      Answer

      The node label whose value defines what counts as one bucket. With hostname, every node is its own bucket, so anti-affinity means "never two matching pods on the same node" (3.13).

    9. Requests vs limits: name the cgroup mechanism behind each, and the two opposite failure symptoms.
      Answer

      requests.cpu → cpu.weight (share under contention) and scheduler bookkeeping only; limits.cpu → cpu.max CFS quota → throttling (slow, zero restarts, nr_throttled climbing); limits.memory → memory.max → kernel OOM kill (exit 137, OOMKilled, restarts). CPU over = slow; memory over = dead (3.16).

    10. Derive the QoS class: a pod with one container that sets requests.memory = limits.memory but no CPU values at all.
      Answer

      Burstable. Guaranteed requires requests == limits for both cpu and memory in every container. Derived, never declared: read it at .status.qosClass (3.17).

    11. You find pods with status Evicted and the node has a MemoryPressure condition. Which eviction machinery was it, and did PDBs apply?
      Answer

      Kubelet node-pressure eviction — a local decision against thresholds (memory.available < 100Mi etc.). PDBs did not apply; they only bind API-initiated eviction (drain). Hard-threshold kills also skip the grace period (3.18).

    12. An HPA targets 50% CPU; one pod currently averages 240%. How many replicas next, and why does scale-down take ~5 minutes?
      Answer

      desired = ceil(1 × 240/50) = 5, applied on the next 15-second loop. Scale-down uses the highest recommendation over a 300s stabilization window, so calm must persist five minutes (3.20).

    13. You edit a ConfigMap. Which consumers see the change, and which never will?
      Answer

      Volume mounts update within ~a minute via the atomic ..data symlink flip. Env vars never (baked at container creation) and subPath mounts never (bind-mounted past the symlink). Fixes: rollout restart, or Kustomize's configMapGenerator hash-rename (3.22, 3.26).

    14. Where does Helm store release history, and what's the classic helm upgrade value-loss trap?
      Answer

      As Secrets named sh.helm.release.v1.<release>.v<N> in the release namespace — one per revision; rollback re-applies one. Upgrading without repeating --set/-f reverts those values to chart defaults (fresh render, not a patch) — repeat them or use --reuse-values (3.24, 3.25).
