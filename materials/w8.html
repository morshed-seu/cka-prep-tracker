    Week 8 — Mock exams & execution

    No new material this week — that's a feature, not a gap. Everything you'll be asked on exam day, you've built, broken and fixed at least once in weeks 0–7. Week 8 converts that knowledge into calm, timed execution: one more killer.sh run, this site's original mock exam, a final pass over the lifecycle classics, and the exam-day mechanics that decide whether your knowledge actually lands as points. Ticking done here also ticks the tracker.

    read task + set context→solve→verify — prove it works→≤6 min? next : flag & skip→second pass by weight
    New this week · the original mock exam This site ships its own 16-task, 120-minute mock built for your 3-VM lab: mock/exam-1 (weighted like the real curriculum — 30% troubleshooting, 25% architecture, 20% networking, 15% workloads, 10% storage — with a countdown timer, a setup script that plants the broken objects, and a graded solutions page with a 66-point pass line). Run it mid-week between 8.1 and your weak-domain drills (8.2): killer.sh measures you against their environment, the mock measures you against yours.

    Final mocks

    8.1

    killer.sh session 2 — same questions, this run is about speed

    Why it matters

    Killer.sh is the timed practice exam that comes bundled free with your CKA registration: two separate 36-hour access windows, each good for one full run at real-exam-style questions on a live cluster. Session 2 is deliberately identical to session 1 (7.14) — same questions, same environment. That sameness is the point: it turns this run into a controlled experiment. Since nothing about the material changed between session 1 and now, any difference in your outcome this time is entirely you — your speed, your verification discipline, your nerve under a countdown, not your knowledge. Finishing known material with 20+ minutes to spare is the strongest single predictor that the real exam's unknown material will still fit inside 120 minutes.

    Steps
    1. Activate session 2 early in the week (Monday/Tuesday), leaving room for 8.2's targeted drills afterwards. Same 36-hour window rules as before — pick a clear 2-hour block.
    2. Full exam conditions again: one monitor, no notes, only kubernetes.io docs open. The per-task ritual (8.6) on every single task, even the ones you remember.
    3. Targets for this run: every task attempted, every attempted task verified, finish line at 100 minutes. You saw these questions three weeks ago — hesitation, not knowledge, is what you're timing.
    4. Afterwards, compare against your session-1 numbers from 7.14: attempted / verified / flagged. The delta is your week-7 training effect, and whatever still ran over budget goes straight onto 8.2's drill list.
    Gotchas

    Remembering an answer is not the same as being fast at typing it — don't skip verification because "you know it's right"; the habit you rehearse this week is the habit you'll run on adrenaline next week. And if session 2 somehow goes worse than session 1, the cause is almost always pacing (sunk time on one task), not regression — check where the minutes went before doubting your preparation.

    8.2

    Weak-domain drills, final round — run the list 7.16 wrote

    Why it matters

    A "domain" is just one of the exam's content areas — troubleshooting, storage, RBAC, networking, and so on — each worth a different slice of the total score. In 7.16 you scored every domain red/yellow/green from hard evidence (your sprint times, your killer.sh misses) and wrote down week 8's drill list from that scoring. This checkpoint is that list, executed, nothing more. Reviewing material you're already good at feels productive and is worth nothing on exam day; this hour goes only to the reds.

    Steps
    1. Take the 7.16 list — typically 2–4 items, each naming a concrete drill: a W6 fault script to re-run, a 7.13 sprint task to repeat, a lesson lab to redo cold.
    2. Re-run each drill under time, notes closed. A drill counts as closed when you finish inside target and verify without prompting yourself.
    3. This is also the slot for this site's 16-task mock exam if you haven't run it yet — it exercises every domain on your own cluster and grades to the real 66% line. Its per-task rubric tells you exactly which domain leaked points.
    4. Anything that fails its drill twice: stop drilling, re-read that lesson's concept section once, slowly. At this stage a repeated failure is usually one missing idea, not missing practice.
    Gotchas

    Don't let a red domain pull you into re-reading whole weeks — you're closing specific gaps, not re-studying. And keep total drill time honest: ~2 focused hours a day maximum this week. Tired repetitions teach sloppiness, and sloppiness is exactly what verification habits exist to catch.

    8.3

    The lifecycle rehearsal: backup → break → restore → upgrade, one last time

    Why it matters

    Backing up and restoring etcd, and stepping a cluster through a kubeadm upgrade, are the two highest-leverage rituals on the whole exam: they're near-guaranteed to show up, heavily weighted, and — once genuinely learned — pure muscle memory rather than something you reason through under pressure. This rehearsal proves you can run the entire loop with no notes, against a timer. If any single step makes you stop and think, that's the step to drill in isolation until it stops.

    The concept

    Nothing here is new material — this is checkpoints 2.9–2.14 compressed into one timed pass — but it's worth saying the mental model out loud once before you race the clock. Every object you've ever created in this cluster (deployments, secrets, configmaps, all of it) lives in etcd, a small key-value database that the rest of the control plane treats as the single source of truth. "Backing up" means asking etcd to write its entire contents out to one file, called a snapshot; "restoring" means pointing a fresh etcd instance at that file instead of its normal live data. Two tools do this, and they work in opposite modes: etcdctl is online — it talks to a running etcd member over the network, proving it's allowed to ask by presenting the same TLS certificates etcd itself uses — while etcdutl is offline — it only ever reads and writes snapshot files sitting on disk, with no running etcd process and no certs involved at all. Because a restore is an etcdutl operation, it never touches the live etcd; instead it materializes a brand-new data directory on disk, and only afterwards do you tell the running etcd (a static pod — a pod the kubelet starts directly from a manifest file, not one created through the apiserver) to use that new directory, by editing its manifest. Upgrades follow their own fixed order: the kubeadm tool gets upgraded first, then the control plane components, then every node's kubelet — one Kubernetes minor version at a time — and each node is drained (its pods safely evicted elsewhere) before its own kubelet is touched. If you can narrate those two ideas — the online/offline tool split, and the upgrade order — while your hands are typing, you're done learning; everything left in this checkpoint is speed.

    Lab
    1. Start a 12-minute timer for steps 2–6 — that's generous exam pace for a backup+restore task. First give the restore something concrete to prove: on cp, create a marker deployment, then ask etcd to write its full snapshot file. 
      kubectl create deployment marker --image=nginx
sudo ETCDCTL_API=3 etcdctl snapshot save /root/rehearsal.db \
  --endpoints=https://127.0.0.1:2379 \
  --cacert=/etc/kubernetes/pki/etcd/ca.crt \
  --cert=/etc/kubernetes/pki/etcd/server.crt \
  --key=/etc/kubernetes/pki/etcd/server.key
      Snapshot saved at /root/rehearsal.db
    2. A snapshot command can report success and still be useless — wrong endpoint, empty file — so check its actual contents before trusting it with your restore. Reading a file needs no live etcd and no certs, which is why this is an etcdutl job, not etcdctl: 
      sudo etcdutl snapshot status /root/rehearsal.db -w table
      | HASH | REVISION | TOTAL KEYS | TOTAL SIZE |
| ...  |     ...  |  ~1200    |   ~4 MB   |   ← 0 keys / tiny file = wrong file
    3. Now do real damage, so the restore has real stakes: delete both the marker you just made and something the cluster actually depends on, since both predate the snapshot: 
      kubectl delete deployment marker
kubectl -n kube-system delete configmap coredns
    4. Restore the snapshot into a directory that doesn't exist yet. etcdutl deliberately refuses to write into a directory that already holds data — that refusal is a safety rail against silently clobbering a working etcd, not a bug to work around: 
      sudo etcdutl snapshot restore /root/rehearsal.db \
  --data-dir /var/lib/etcd-from-backup
    5. The restored data is just sitting on disk right now — nothing is using it yet. Tell the running etcd to switch over by editing its static pod manifest (the file the kubelet watches and rebuilds the pod from whenever it changes), repointing the hostPath volume — the line that says which directory on the host gets mounted into the container — rather than the etcd command-line flags: 
      sudo vim /etc/kubernetes/manifests/etcd.yaml
  # volumes: → hostPath: → path: /var/lib/etcd  →  /var/lib/etcd-from-backup

      The kubelet notices the manifest change and restarts etcd on the new data; the apiserver drops and returns within a minute or two.

    6. Prove the restore actually worked, not just that the manifest edit was accepted — both casualties should be back: 
      kubectl get deployment marker
kubectl -n kube-system get configmap coredns
      NAME     READY   UP-TO-DATE   AVAILABLE
marker   1/1     1            1
NAME      DATA
coredns   1
    7. Timer check, then the upgrade half — same rehearsal, different target. First see whether a newer v1.35 patch has shipped since week 2: 
      sudo apt-get update -qq && apt-cache madison kubeadm | head -3

      If a newer 1.35.x exists: run the full one-node ritual on cp for real — upgrade kubeadm → sudo kubeadm upgrade plan → sudo kubeadm upgrade apply v1.35.x → drain cp → upgrade kubelet+kubectl → restart kubelet → uncordon. If you're already on the newest patch: recite the sequence out loud anyway, run sudo kubeadm upgrade plan (it must come back clean), and — if you want the full-contact version — roll the VMs back to your pre-upgrade snapshots from 2.8 and redo the entire 1.34→1.35 climb from 2.13–2.14.

    8. Clean up the rehearsal debris: 
      kubectl delete deployment marker
sudo rm /root/rehearsal.db     # keep /var/lib/etcd-from-backup — it IS the live data dir now
    Verify

    Steps 2–6 fit inside 12 minutes with zero doc lookups; kubectl get nodes shows all three Ready afterwards; and you can recite the upgrade order (kubeadm → plan → apply → drain → kubelet → uncordon; control plane before workers; one minor at a time) without hesitation.

    Gotchas

    The three classic restore fumbles, one last time — each one because the online/offline split gets blurred under pressure: cert flags belong only to snapshot save (online, talking to a real etcd), never to restore (offline, just files) — etcdutl doesn't even accept them; restore must target a directory name that doesn't exist yet, or it refuses outright; and the actual switch-over happens in the manifest's hostPath volume line, not in an etcd command-line flag — editing a --data-dir flag while leaving the volume pointed at the old path is the trap that looks right on screen and silently does nothing, because the container never sees the new directory. If the apiserver doesn't come back after the manifest edit, the etcd container itself is the thing to check first: sudo crictl ps -a | grep etcd, then read the fresh container's logs — it's almost always a typo'd path.

    Docs

    Docs search: "Operating etcd clusters for Kubernetes" (backup/restore) and "Upgrading kubeadm clusters" — bookmark both mentally; on the exam you may open them, but this rehearsal is about not needing to.

    8.4

    Prune your notes to the truly non-obvious

    Why it matters

    The exam room gives you nothing to write on and nothing brought in — no notes, no cheat sheet, nothing but the docs tab (8.5). So whatever's in your notes today has exactly one path to being useful on exam day: it has to move from the page into your head before Thursday. A 10-page dump transfers nothing, because you won't re-read all 10 pages enough times for any of it to stick; a half-page of the genuinely non-obvious transfers completely, because you will. Pruning is the last act of studying, not busywork before it.

    Steps
    1. Lay out the four artifacts: your triage sheet (6.23), the commands card, your lookup log from 7.13, and any margin notes.
    2. Delete every line you'd reproduce from understanding anyway — if you can derive it, it's not a note, it's clutter. Typical survivors: the etcdctl cert-flag trio, the jsonpath range/filter shapes, NetworkPolicy from/to indentation, the drain flag pair, --record-style flags you always forget.
    3. What remains should fit half a page. Read it once per day through Thursday — that's the entire remaining study plan for facts.
    4. Cross-check the survivors against the exam-day sheet — anything about process (rituals, time rules) lives there, not in your facts list. Facts and process are rehearsed differently.
    Gotchas

    The pruning trap is sentimentality: keeping a note because it cost you pain to learn. Pain is not a retention strategy — if the etcd restore sequence is truly automatic after 8.3, delete it from the sheet and trust the drill. The opposite trap: pruning things you wish were automatic but aren't. Be honest; the 8.3 timer doesn't lie.

    Exam mechanics

    8.5

    Internalize the format: what the two hours actually look like

    Why it matters

    Every mechanical surprise on exam day costs minutes and calm — not because the surprise is hard, but because working out "wait, how does this even work" eats time you budgeted for actual tasks. There should be zero surprises: the format is fully documented in advance, and by now you've rehearsed it twice in killer.sh and once in the mock. This lesson is the final walk-through of the room you'll actually be working in.

    The concept

    The numbers first: roughly 15–20 weighted tasks, 120 minutes, 66% to pass. "Weighted" means the tasks aren't worth equal points — a networking task might be worth several times a small workload fix — and "performance-only" means the grader only looks at the final state of your clusters afterward, with no idea how you got there; there's no such thing as the wrong process as long as the objects end up correct, and partial credit exists per task for getting partway there.

    The environment is the less familiar part. You don't run the exam on your own machine directly: you connect through the PSI Secure Browser — PSI is the company that administers and proctors the exam, and their "Secure Browser" is a locked-down app that blocks other software on your machine while it's running — to a remote XFCE desktop. (XFCE is just a lightweight Linux desktop environment — the windows, taskbar and file manager sitting on top of Linux, nothing exotic.) That remote machine, not your laptop, is what your terminal and your one allowed Firefox tab actually run inside. That has consequences that catch people off guard if they haven't rehearsed it: your local shell aliases, bookmarks and clipboard manager don't exist there, because none of that machine is yours — set up fresh aliases in your first two minutes instead (8.12); copy/paste does work, but through the remote desktop's own conventions rather than your OS's, and each task's on-screen text includes click-to-copy buttons for exact names and paths that you should use instead of retyping by hand and risking a typo; and everything feels roughly 200ms slower than practicing at home, purely from screen-sharing latency — which is exactly why your speed training (8.1, 8.7) was built with margin to spare.

    The one allowed reference is a second Firefox tab, inside that same remote desktop, restricted to official documentation: kubernetes.io/docs (and its blog), helm.sh/docs, and — specific to CKA — the Gateway API docs at gateway-api.sigs.k8s.io (Gateway API is the newer, more expressive way of configuring ingress-style traffic routing that's gradually replacing plain Ingress objects). Searching within those sites is fine; clicking through to a result that leads off those domains is not. Each task states which cluster and context it applies to, and its header usually shows the exact context-switch command ready to copy — which is exactly what 8.6's first ritual is built around.

    Steps
    1. Read the two official pages end-to-end once this week: the CKA Important Instructions and Frequently Asked Questions (linked from your Linux Foundation portal — same place as the killer.sh sessions).
    2. Print or save the exam-day sheet — it compresses this group's five lessons onto one page; final-48h review uses it, not the lessons.
    3. Confirm the mechanics knowledge is real: you should be able to answer, cold — how many tasks? how long? pass mark? which docs tabs are legal? what runs locally vs remotely? (All five are in the quiz below.)
    Gotchas

    The remote-desktop model means your machine's job is only to run the PSI browser well: close everything else, wire your network if you can, and don't plan on your local terminal fonts, aliases or clipboard history — they won't be there. Set up the environment's own aliases in your first two minutes instead (the 0.9 block, from memory — it's four lines).

    Docs

    Portal search: "Important Instructions CKA" and "Certification resources allowed" on docs.linuxfoundation.org — the allowed-resources list is the authoritative version of what you may open.

    8.6

    The two per-task rituals: context first, ssh discipline always

    Why it matters

    A kubectl "context" is just a saved bundle — which cluster, which credentials, which default namespace — that a command uses without you having to spell all three out every time; switching context is how one kubectl binary talks to several different clusters without being reconfigured by hand. The single most expensive class of exam error is solving a task perfectly in the wrong place anyway — right answer, wrong cluster, wrong namespace, or typed into a node's shell instead of your own. All three are prevented by two rituals that cost about five seconds each, every single time. By Friday they must be reflexes you do without deciding to, not checklist items you remember to run.

    Steps
    1. Ritual one — context, then namespace. Every task begins with the exact kubectl config use-context … line shown at the top of the task. Run it every time, even when you believe you're already there — belief is how points die. Then check whether the task names a namespace and put it in the command (-n) or switch the context default (kubectl config set-context --current --namespace=…) — pick one habit and keep it; mixing the two is how "created it in default" happens.
    2. Ritual two — ssh in, do the node work, exit immediately. Some tasks say "on node01" — ssh there, do exactly that step, and leave. The failure mode: staying on the node, then running kubectl commands that either fail (no kubeconfig there) or, worse, quietly succeed against the wrong thing. Your prompt tells you where you are — glance at it before every command block. Rehearsed in the lab all along: multipass shell node01 … work … exit.
    3. Burn both in during the mock and killer.sh session 2: physically run the context line on all 16 tasks even though your lab has one context — the point is making the motion unconditional.
    Gotchas

    The context line is per-task, not per-sitting: tasks jump between clusters, and the one time you skip the ritual is statistically the time the cluster changed. And after any ssh work, verify you're back: if your next kubectl command errors with a connection refused or an unknown context, you're still on the node — exit, don't debug a problem you don't have.

    8.7

    Decide your time strategy now — and make it arithmetic, not vibes

    Why it matters

    Do the arithmetic once, plainly: 120 minutes over roughly 16 tasks is 7.5 minutes each on average — and that's before subtracting reading time, verification, and the ~200ms-slower remote-desktop tax from 8.5. Nobody actually solves every task in one clean pass at that pace; the people who fail despite knowing the material are almost always the ones who let one sticky task quietly eat twenty minutes that belonged to three other tasks. The strategy for handling that has to be decided this week, on paper, with a clear head — because in the exam room, under adrenaline and a visible countdown, you will only execute decisions you already made in advance, not ones you try to reason out in the moment.

    Steps
    1. Adopt the two-pass rule: first pass, nothing gets more than 6 minutes. Solve-and-verify what flows; the moment you're stuck or the clock hits 6, flag the task in the exam UI, note one word about why ("netpol selector?", "restore path"), and move.
    2. First pass done (~90 minutes in a 16-task exam), you know the whole board. Second pass: flagged tasks in descending weight order — an 8%-task half-solved outranks a 4%-task perfected.
    3. Budget the last 5 minutes for nothing new: sweep your flags, confirm every attempted task was verified, resist the urge to "improve" working answers.
    4. Rehearse the rule where it's hard: in the mock and in killer.sh session 2, actually abandon tasks at 6 minutes. Training the leaving is the whole point — solving was trained in weeks 1–7.
    Gotchas

    Partial credit changes the math: a task you half-do (created the PVC, pod still Pending) scores something, a task you never reached scores zero — which is why the first pass must touch everything. The other trap is the reverse: polishing. Once verification passes, the task is over; every extra minute there is a minute stolen from a flagged task at full point value.

    8.8

    Verify as you go — the habit that converts answers into points

    Why it matters

    Remember from 8.5: the exam grades the resulting cluster state, not your effort or your intent — so a solution with one silent typo scores exactly zero, and nothing in the exam UI will tell you that happened. The verification habit, drilled since week 0 one lesson at a time, is what separates "I answered every task" from "I scored every task I answered" — and those two numbers can differ by a lot. This lesson is the final, compressed form of that habit: one proof command per task shape, run automatically, every time.

    The concept

    Every task type has a natural one-liner that proves it from the consumer's side rather than the creator's side — not "does the object exist" but "does the thing actually work for whoever would use it":

    You just…Prove it with
    created/edited a workloadkubectl get deploy,pods -n … → Ready counts, then rollout status
    changed an imagerollout status + kubectl describe pod … | grep Image:
    made a Service/Ingress/HTTPRoutecurl/wget it from a throwaway pod or the node — an HTTP 200, not a warm feeling
    wrote RBACkubectl auth can-i … --as=… for the allow and a deny
    bound storagekubectl get pvc -n … → Bound, pod Running
    fixed a node/componentkubectl get nodes / get pods -n kube-system — Ready, and stays Ready for 30s
    saved a file the task asked forcat it — right path, right content, non-empty
    Steps
    1. Run the mock (or session 2) with a hard personal rule: a task isn't "done" until its proof command has run and shown the right thing. Track your compliance honestly — it should be 16/16.
    2. For file-output tasks (common!): re-read the task's exact path and re-cat the file. Wrong path is the classic zero-point non-error.
    3. Fold the table above into your fingers, not your notes — it's also on the exam-day sheet for the final read.
    Gotchas

    Verification catches your typos, but read what it says — a rollout status that hangs is telling you the fix didn't take (image typo, probe failing), and that information is worth more mid-exam than after. If a proof command fails, you haven't lost time; you've been handed the task back while it's still worth points.

    8.9

    Prep the room and pass check-in like you've done it before

    Why it matters

    PSI (the company proctoring your exam, from 8.5) runs a strict, slightly tedious, entirely predictable check-in process before every session — a government-ID check and a webcam pan of your room, mainly to confirm you're who you say you are and that you're not getting outside help. Predictable means it's either a total non-event, because you prepared for exactly what's coming, or a self-inflicted stress spike right before the most important two hours of your quarter, because you didn't. Everything in this checkpoint is decided and prepared the day before, on purpose — not discovered live during check-in.

    Steps
    1. The desk: completely clear except your machine — no papers, second keyboard, or gadgets. One monitor only (unplug externals if you use a laptop lid-open setup). Phone out of reach after check-in starts.
    2. You and the room: government ID ready; a room you can close, alone — interruptions can end the session; the proctor will ask for a webcam pan of the desk, under it, and the room, so make that boring. Water in a clear container only. No headphones; talking to yourself is also flagged — practice thinking silently in the mock if you're a mutterer.
    3. The machine: run the official PSI system check once more the day before (link is in your exam-portal entry), on the same network and machine you'll use. Close every background app; kill notifications; wired network beats Wi-Fi if you have the option.
    4. The clock: check-in opens 30 minutes before your slot — take all of it. Log in immediately when it opens; ID verification and the room pan eat 10–20 minutes, and starting the actual exam unrushed is worth points.
    Gotchas

    Two avoidable disasters: a corporate laptop whose management software blocks the PSI browser (the system check catches this — run it on time to have a plan B), and an "almost empty" desk that turns into ten minutes of proctor-directed furniture moving on camera. Also re-read the ID rule: the name on your exam registration must match the ID exactly — fix mismatches with support this week, not at check-in.

    Docs

    Portal search: "PSI online proctoring requirements" and the CKA "Important Instructions" page — the definitive room/ID/desk rules, straight from the people who enforce them.

    Final 48 hours

    8.10

    Day −2: light review only — the plan is confidence maintenance

    Why it matters

    Two days out, your score is already effectively set by eight weeks of work behind you — the only variables genuinely still in play are how rested and how calm you are on the day. New topics can now only hurt: anything you'd learn for the first time today would be shallow and unreliable under pressure, and "discovering" a gap two days out that you can't properly close in that time manufactures real anxiety for zero point value in return.

    Steps
    1. Total study time today: ≤1 hour. Read your pruned notes (8.4), the triage sheet, and type the etcd backup/restore pair once from memory in the lab — that's the whole session.
    2. Do the logistics sweep while you're calm: PSI system check re-run (8.9), ID on the desk, exam-slot time zone double-checked against your calendar, tomorrow and exam-day meals planned.
    3. If anxiety wants a job, give it a harmless one: re-run one fault script you've beaten before and beat it again. Rehearsing a win is legitimate psychology; hunting new material is not.
    Gotchas

    The classic day−2 mistake is one last full mock "to be sure". A tired 61% two days out tells you nothing about Saturday except that you were tired — but it will sit in your head anyway. The mocks are done; their information is already in your drill list. Trust the eight weeks.

    8.11

    Day −1: rest is the strategy, not the reward

    Why it matters

    A 120-minute performance exam is cognitively closer to a sports event than a written quiz — it demands sustained working memory, focused attention and calm under a visible clock, and all three of those are the first things a short night of sleep takes away. Sleep is measurably worth more exam points tomorrow than any content you could cram today; that's not a wellness platitude tacked onto the syllabus, it's straightforward exam strategy given what the exam actually measures.

    Steps
    1. Flash review only, 20 minutes maximum, before mid-afternoon: pruned notes + the exam-day sheet once through. Then the books close for real.
    2. Prepare the room to 8.9's spec today: desk cleared, monitor situation settled, ID out, water glass chosen. Tomorrow-you should walk into a finished set.
    3. Normal evening, normal food, screen-light down early, normal bedtime — not an artificially early one you'll lie awake through. The goal is an ordinary night, executed well.
    4. If your brain insists on rehearsing: run the per-task ritual (8.6) in your head once — context line, namespace, solve, verify, next — and leave it there. That loop is genuinely useful to fall asleep to.
    Gotchas

    Beware the "one quick thing" spiral — checking a single fact at 9pm has a way of becoming ninety minutes of tabs. If a fact feels urgent tonight, write it on a sticky note for the morning's 20-minute window instead; it will look a lot less urgent by then. It always does.

    8.12

    Exam day: check in early, then one task at a time

    Why it matters

    Everything that was going to be trained is trained; today is purely execution of habits you already own. The two hours will feel fast, and occasionally unfair — a weird task, a laggy minute, a topic you happen to dislike. None of that is signal about how you're doing; it's just what a timed exam feels like from the inside, for everyone. You've broken and fixed this exact system fifty times over eight weeks — the exam is just once more, with a proctor watching instead of no one.

    Steps
    1. Morning: eat normally, 20-minute glance at the pruned notes if you want it, water bottle (clear) filled, bathroom before check-in — the timer doesn't stop for breaks and any break costs proctor re-verification.
    2. Log in the moment check-in opens (30 minutes early) and let the ID/room verification take however long it takes. You budgeted for it; boredom here is the plan working.
    3. First two minutes inside: set up your shell (the 0.9 alias block from memory), open the docs tab, breathe once. Then run the machine you built: context → namespace → solve → verify → ≤6 min → next (8.6/8.7/8.8), first pass through everything, second pass by weight.
    4. When something rattles you — and something will — the reset is physical and rehearsed: hands off the keyboard, one breath, re-read the task text slowly, run the context line again, continue. Ten seconds, fully recoverable.
    5. Afterwards: results arrive by email within 24 hours. Close the laptop and go do something human; you've earned it either way.
    Gotchas

    Don't grade yourself mid-exam ("that's three I've flagged, I'm failing") — you have no idea how the weights fall, and the arithmetic anxiety costs real minutes. Flags are your system working, not a score. And if the worst happens: you have a retake, most narrow misses pass round two comfortably, and the tracker's last note tells you exactly how to book it. That safety net is precisely why you can afford to play today calm.

    Week 8 recall quiz — exam mechanics edition

    These are the facts that must be automatic on Saturday. Answer out loud before revealing.

    1. The format numbers: how many tasks, how long, what pass mark, graded how?
      Answer

      ~15–20 weighted tasks, 120 minutes, 66% to pass, performance-based — graded on resulting cluster state, with partial credit per task (8.5).

    2. Where do your terminal and browser actually run during the exam, and what follows from that?
      Answer

      Inside a remote XFCE desktop reached through the PSI Secure Browser — so local bookmarks, aliases, and clipboard tools don't exist there; use the task UI's click-to-copy, set up aliases fresh, and expect a small latency tax (8.5).

    3. Which documentation may your one extra Firefox tab open?
      Answer

      kubernetes.io/docs (and blog), helm.sh/docs, and — CKA-specific — the Gateway API docs at gateway-api.sigs.k8s.io. On-site search is fine; following results off those domains is not (8.5).

    4. The two per-task rituals, verbatim.
      Answer

      (1) Run the task's kubectl config use-context … line first, every task, then handle the namespace deliberately. (2) If the task needs a node: ssh in, do exactly that work, exit immediately — and glance at the prompt before every command block (8.6).

    5. Recite the two-pass time strategy, including both numbers.
      Answer

      First pass: every task, ≤6 minutes each — flag and skip anything sticky. Second pass: flagged tasks in descending weight order. Last 5 minutes: sweep flags and verifications, nothing new (8.7).

    6. Why does the first pass have to touch every task?
      Answer

      Partial credit: a half-done task scores something, an unreached task scores zero. Touching everything converts the maximum number of tasks into non-zero scores before perfectionism spends the clock (8.7).

    7. You just created a Service the task asked for. What's the verification — and what isn't?
      Answer

      Consume it: curl/wget the Service from a throwaway pod (or node, for NodePort) and see real HTTP payload. "The object exists in kubectl get svc" is not verification — selectors and ports can still be wrong (8.8).

    8. etcd: which tool takes the snapshot, which restores it, and which one needs the cert flags?
      Answer

      etcdctl snapshot save — online, needs --endpoints + cacert/cert/key. etcdutl snapshot restore (and status) — offline, file-only, no certs, must target a fresh data dir; then repoint the manifest's hostPath volume (8.3).

    9. The kubeadm upgrade order, one breath.
      Answer

      Upgrade kubeadm itself → upgrade plan → upgrade apply on the control plane → then per node: drain, upgrade kubelet (+kubectl), restart kubelet, uncordon. Control plane before workers, one minor version at a time (8.3).

    10. Name four PSI room rules that are checked at check-in.
      Answer

      Clear desk; single monitor; government ID matching the registration name; webcam pan of desk/room; clear water container only; phone away; alone in a closable room. (Any four — but know all of them; 8.9.)

    11. When does check-in open and why do you use all of it?
      Answer

      30 minutes before the slot. ID verification plus the room pan routinely eat 10–20 minutes; logging in at the bell means starting your 120 minutes flustered — the cheapest points you'll ever buy are the ones from starting calm (8.9/8.12).

    12. What does a failed first attempt actually cost you?
      Answer

      A few weeks: the exam includes a retake when eligible, and narrow misses overwhelmingly pass round two after drilling only the domains that hurt. Knowing the net exists is part of why you can execute calmly (8.12).
